## Intro (Steel → Broadcasting → AI)

Osaka Hoso's predecessor, Maruichi Kosan Co., Ltd., was founded in 1945 as a steel company in Osaka. From Itatebori to Sakai-Sanbo, Edobori, then to Hokkō-Tsuneyoshi — moving with the industry, for eighty years the company traded in steel and its alloys, while building a solid foundation in real estate.

Then we met a broadcaster. Radio Osaka (OBC) — on the air since 1958, a public-interest broadcaster that has carried Osaka's airwaves for more than half a century. In a rapidly shifting media landscape, the station needed a new structure for its next era. We didn't just invest and watch from the sidelines. We walked in as members of the team and committed to building that next era together.

In 2026, we changed our company name to Osaka Hoso Co., Ltd. This was not a rebrand. It was a declaration: we staked the name of our 80-year-old enterprise on a commitment to walk forward with Radio Osaka, all in.

Our vision for Radio Osaka is transformation into a content IP studio. Programs that flow through the airwaves and vanish — replaced by content that accumulates as assets the more you create. Starting from radio, building IP that endures and is loved.

We started that transformation at home. We rebuilt Radio Osaka — from operations and accounting to on-air programming — with AI, using our own hands. "The older the organization, the bigger the AI impact." This conviction, forged on the foundation of eighty years of trust, is now our core business: AI/DX transformation for companies.

From steel, to broadcasting, to AI. The shape of the business changes. But we stay rooted in Osaka, and we protect what must be protected, no matter what.

## Founder Gentaro Kato — His life, in brief

Born in 1906 in Toyooka, Hyōgo Prefecture. At fifteen, he entered the Osaka steel firm of Yasutaro Kitajima, stepping into the world of iron. Over twenty years under Kitajima, he sharpened his judgment and nerve, and earned a reputation for unwavering loyalty.

In October 1945 — one month after Japan's surrender — he went independent at age thirty-nine. From a single desk, he built Kato Shoten, which he later incorporated as Maruichi Kosan. The company surged through Japan's high-growth era, and Kato established himself as a pillar of the Kansai steel distribution industry. From founding to death, the company never posted a loss.

Stern but paternal with his employees, he cared for his former mentor Yasutaro Kitajima — who had fallen on hard times — for thirty years. Those who knew called it a story of profound gratitude.

He died in 1979, at seventy-three, still serving as president. His legacy passed to his son Katsuhiro, grandson Masataka, and today's leader Yorihiko — and eighty years on, the company now writes its newest chapter as Osaka Hoso.
