Why "Old Companies" Benefit Most from AI — Lessons from Cambodian Villages and an Osaka Broadcaster

Kengo Kitaura（Director, Osaka Hoso Co., Ltd.） June 4, 2026 Last updated: June 4, 2026

Key takeaways

  • "We're an old company, AI isn't for us" is exactly backwards. The more paper-heavy, the more people-dependent, the more set in its ways — the larger the room for AI to make a dramatic difference.
  • Fourteen years in Cambodian farming villages — building a system to bring farmers with no records and no credit history into the financial system — and the structural problem I saw there was identical to a broadcaster founded in 1958.
  • Before you deploy any AI tool, the work is turning tribal knowledge into explicit knowledge and doing the "upstream design." It can't be done from a desk — and the further from digital the site is, the bigger the change.

"AI is for tech companies" — the biggest misconception

When we talk about AI, most business owners say the same thing:

"We're an old company. AI isn't for us."

It's the exact opposite. The more paper-heavy, people-dependent, and set-in-its-ways an organization is, the more dramatically AI can change it.

Digital-native tech companies are already optimized. There's less for AI to cut. Paper-and-stamp organizations, on the other hand, are sitting on mountains of redundant transcription, tribal knowledge locked in individuals' heads, and rules that haven't changed in thirty years.

For AI, that's a gold mine.

What I learned in Cambodian villages

From 2009, I spent roughly fourteen years in Cambodia. In 2014, I founded AGRIBUDDY, an agri-fintech company, and served as CEO.

In Cambodian farming villages, farmers keep no records. Harvest volumes, sale prices, costs — everything lives inside their heads. No records means no credit. No credit means no insurance. No insurance means no financial services.

The structural parallel to Japanese SME accounting is striking.

"Only that one person knows." "It's written on paper somewhere but nobody's organized it." "There are rules, but they've never been documented." As long as knowledge lives inside people's heads, technology can't function.

In Cambodia, we started by building the recording mechanism itself. A system that lets farmers create records naturally. Input cost pushed as close to zero as possible. That design became the foundation for everything else.

The same thing happened at Radio Osaka

In 2026, I entered the management reform of Radio Osaka (OBC) — a broadcaster Osaka Hoso invested in.

A station founded in 1958. Thirty years of accounting data sitting in a proprietary system. The data existed, but it wasn't in a form that could drive management decisions. We were "driving a car with no gauges."

On top of that, paper vouchers everywhere. Every transaction initiated on paper, passed through multiple approval stamps. The same numbers transcribed from paper to system to Excel, over and over.

When I saw this situation, I was immediately reminded of Cambodian villages. The structure of the problem was identical.

"The further from digital, the greater the opportunity for digitalization." The conviction I'd formed in Cambodian farming villages was proven again in an Osaka broadcasting studio.

What needs to happen before you bring in AI tools

We frequently hear from companies: "We want to automate with AI." But when we dig in, the stage before automation hasn't been set in most cases.

You want to automate invoice processing. But where invoices come from, who reviews them, and how approvals work — none of it is written down.

You want to automate compensation calculations. But "this person gets this rate, that edge case works differently" — exceptions everywhere, documented nowhere, sustained by years of habit alone.

To automate any of this, you first need to turn implicit knowledge into explicit knowledge.

Nobody wants to do it. It's unglamorous and slow. But skip it, and even the best AI tool in the world will spin its wheels.

Upstream design is everything

AI excels at processing data it's been given, at high speed. AI struggles to judge what data it should be given in the first place.

A river analogy makes this clear: someone stands upstream and decides what flows down; downstream, AI processes it at high speed. That division of roles is the essence of effective AI adoption.

Plenty of companies sell AI. But surprisingly few can actually do the upstream design — the work of deciding what goes in and what comes out. The reason is simple: upstream work requires being on-site. You can't do it by looking at data alone. Unless you feel how a company actually operates, you can't see what needs to be designed.

This is not work that can be done remotely from an office.

What actually changed at OBC

When we started from upstream design at Radio Osaka, here's what happened:

  • 162 chart-of-account items consolidated into 37 categories, then into 7 top-level groups
  • AI auto-generated P&L statements from 30 years / 2.11 million rows of data — deviation from audited financials: 0.01%
  • Every paper voucher and approval stamp eliminated
  • 2,500–3,000 journal entries per month, processed automatically by AI
  • A "digital library" where anyone can ask AI about the company and get an accurate answer instantly

All of this was made possible by AI. But none of it would have worked with AI alone. We walked into the operation, turned tribal knowledge into documentation, and designed the input-output structure. Only then did AI start to function.

"We're too old for this" — the most expensive misconception

If your company "has a lot of paper," "depends on specific individuals," or "has been doing things the same way for decades" — that means your company is where AI has the most impact.

Cambodian farming villages and a broadcaster founded in 1958 were both as far from digital as you can get. And that's precisely where the greatest room for change existed.

Frequently asked questions

Q.Why are "old companies" the ones that benefit most from AI?

Paper-heavy, people-dependent, and set-in-its-ways organizations are sitting on mountains of redundant transcription, tribal knowledge locked inside individuals' heads, and rules that haven't changed in thirty years. That's exactly the kind of work AI can automate and compress. Digital-native tech companies, by contrast, are already optimized — there's much less for AI to cut.

Q.What did Cambodian farming villages and the Osaka broadcaster have in common?

Both were stuck in the same state: the records existed, but they weren't in a form that could actually be used. Cambodian farmers had harvest volumes, sale prices, and costs — all in their heads. The radio station had thirty years of accounting data — none of it in a form that could drive business decisions. "Only that one person knows." "It's on paper somewhere but nobody's organized it." "There are rules but they've never been documented." Structurally, exactly the same problem.

Q.What should we do before deploying any AI tool?

Turn the tribal knowledge into explicit knowledge. Where do invoices come from, who reviews them, how do the exceptions in compensation actually work — pull all that out of people's heads and out of "how we've always done it" and write it down. It's slow and unglamorous work. Skip it and no matter how good the AI tool is, it will spin its wheels.

Q.Why does "upstream design" require being on site?

Because looking at the data alone doesn't tell you how the company actually runs. You can't feel how the operation moves from a dashboard. It's work that has to be done on the floor, watching the actual operation — not from an office, remotely staring at data extracts.
