How We Rebuilt a 1958 Broadcaster's Entire Accounting System with AI

Kengo Kitaura（Director, Osaka Hoso Co., Ltd.） June 4, 2026 Last updated: June 4, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Radio Osaka, on the air since 1958, was a "car with a perfect maintenance log but no gauges" — three decades of records, none of them in a form that could actually drive business decisions.
  • We consolidated 162 accounts into 37 categories and 7 groups, then let AI process 2.11 million rows of journal data from thirty years — final variance against the audited statements: 0.01%. Paper vouchers and approval stamps were eliminated, and 2,500–3,000 monthly entries are now AI-automated.
  • The key is "upstream design" — humans decide what goes in and what comes out, then AI does the processing. The older and more paper-heavy the organization, the bigger the gain.

Walking into a broadcaster founded in 1958

Radio Osaka (OBC) is a legacy broadcaster in the Kansai region, on the air since 1958. When Osaka Hoso invested in the station and Yorihiko Kato took the helm as Representative Director and Chairman, I joined the team on the inside.

The first thing Chairman Kato told me was this:

"The numbers are solid. But they're not in a state where I can use them to run the business."

A car with no gauges

All the accounting data was stored in a proprietary system built by a single employee roughly thirty years ago. Every transaction from 1996 to today — all of it. Built in the Windows 95 era, that system had kept records without a single gap for three decades. As a maintenance logbook, it was flawless.

But if you think of it as a car, the situation looked like this:

The maintenance log was perfect. Oil change dates, tire replacement history — all there, thirty years' worth, without a single gap. But there were no gauges. No speedometer. No fuel gauge. No engine temperature readout.

Close the monthly books and a financial statement comes out. But "what to do with these numbers" wasn't intuitive. The records were there, yet nobody could tell where the company was actually heading.

That was the reality of a broadcaster founded in 1958.

The first move: design from the outcome backward

My first step was not "migrate all the data."

I started by defining what Chairman Kato needs to see each month to make management decisions.

Then I worked backward from there, reshaping only the numbers that mattered into a usable form. We call this "outcome-first accounting design."

Specifically, I consolidated 162 chart-of-account items into 37 categories, then grouped those into 7 top-level clusters. Chairman Kato only needs to see seven numbers. "Revenue, costs, profit" — confirmed every month at a glance. Need more detail? Drill into the 37 categories. Need the granular view? The full 162 accounts are still there.

Showing every number is not management. Creating a state where you can see what you need, at the granularity you need, whenever you need it — that is the point.

Processing 2.11 million rows with AI

Thirty years of journal entries. 2.11 million rows. We fed them to AI and auto-generated profit-and-loss statements for the past five fiscal years.

Cross-checked against the actual audited financials, the deviation was 0.01%.

That precision wasn't just because AI is smart. It happened because a human designed the upstream structure first — consolidating 162 accounts into 37 categories. Humans design what goes in; AI processes it. Reverse that order, and this precision would never have materialized.

Every paper voucher and approval stamp — eliminated

At OBC, every transaction was initiated on a paper voucher and passed through multiple approval stamps. Purchase orders, transfers, invoices, expenses — all paper.

"Write on paper → copy → enter → tally on paper again → re-enter in Excel." That chain of redundant transcription was daily life.

We ripped it out entirely.

AI reads receipts and invoices and generates journal entries automatically. Paper approval stamps were replaced with digital authorization. The paper vouchers themselves were abolished.

The result: 2,500–3,000 journal entries per month, processed automatically by AI.

Building a system where everyone's knowledge connects

Alongside the accounting automation, we built something bigger.

We organized the company's vast trove of information — financials, equipment data, sales materials, broadcast performance — into a format AI can read, creating a state where anyone can ask AI a question about the company and get an accurate answer instantly.

"What's the profit trend over the past five years?" — a five-year P&L comparison appears. "Total cost and schedule for equipment upgrades?" — a breakdown by equipment type and timeline.

Beyond gauges — a cockpit where the driver says "show me that number" and it appears instantly. That's what's running at OBC today.

Why "old companies" benefit most from AI

Working through the OBC transformation confirmed something we now hold as a conviction:

The more paper-heavy, people-dependent, and set-in-its-ways an organization is, the more dramatically AI can change it.

Digital-native companies are already optimized — there's less for AI to cut. Paper-and-stamp organizations, on the other hand, are sitting on mountains of redundant transcription, tribal knowledge locked in individuals' heads, and rules unchanged for thirty years. For AI, that's a gold mine.

But dropping in an AI tool alone changes nothing. Unless a human designs the upstream structure — what goes in, what comes out — AI spins its wheels.

Frequently asked questions

Q.How much data did the OBC AI rebuild actually process?

Thirty years' worth — roughly 2.11 million rows of journal data — processed in one pass to auto-generate profit-and-loss statements for the last five fiscal years. The variance against the audited statements was 0.01%.

Q.Did you really eliminate all the paper vouchers and approval stamps?

Yes. Every paper voucher — purchase orders, transfers, billing, expenses — was removed, and the approval stamps were replaced with electronic approvals. 2,500–3,000 journal entries a month now run on AI.

Q.What exactly is "accounting design from the outcome backward"?

It means deciding what numbers the executive actually needs to see every month, then designing the consolidation structure backward from there. At OBC, we collapsed 162 accounts into 37 categories, then grouped those into 7. The detail is still there if you want to drill down — we just optimized the resolution at the level the decision is made.

Q.Can the same approach work for other SMEs?

Yes. Osaka Hoso took what we proved at OBC and packaged it as a service for mid-market companies. The more paper-heavy, people-dependent, and set-in-its-ways the organization, the bigger the effect.
