"AI sounds great. But how much does it actually save?"

When we talk with business owners about AI-powered accounting, there's one question that comes up every single time:

"What's the actual ROI?"

It's the right question. No matter how impressive the technology, business decisions come down to cost and return. In this article, we break down the financial impact of AI accounting using real numbers from our reform of Radio Osaka (OBC) — a broadcaster Osaka Hoso invested in.

First: what accounting actually costs you

Most mid-market companies run their accounting with two to four people. A typical setup looks like this:

  • 3 accounting staff (¥300,000/month each = ¥900,000)
  • 1 accounting manager (¥600,000/month)

That's ¥1.5 million per month, or ¥18 million per year. But that's just base salary.

In reality, employer-side social insurance adds roughly 15% on top. Then there's recruitment cost, training cost, and turnover risk.

The average cost of a single mid-career hire in Japan is approximately ¥800,000 (Recruit data). According to Japan's Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (2024), the national average separation rate is approximately 11.5%. With a four-person team, that means roughly one departure every two years. Each time, you recruit again, spend three-plus months on handover, and the remaining team absorbs the burden.

The true cost of accounting is far larger than what shows up on the payroll.

What changes when AI takes over

Here's what we actually did at OBC, laid out in numbers.

Before: - Every transaction initiated on a paper voucher with multiple approval stamps - "Paper → copy → enter → tally on paper → re-enter in Excel" — a chain of redundant transcription - Monthly close took weeks; management numbers arrived too late for decisions - Multiple processes that only specific individuals could handle

After: - AI reads receipts and invoices, generates journal entries automatically - Every paper voucher and approval stamp — eliminated - 2,500–3,000 journal entries per month, processed automatically - Management dashboard with real-time visibility into financial performance - Anyone can run the books and get the same result — zero dependency on specific individuals

This transformation dramatically reduces headcount requirements. A four-person team (3 staff + 1 manager) can be replaced by a single person operating the AI system.

Approximately ¥16.9 million saved per year

Here's the breakdown:

Item Annual amount
3 accounting staff salaries ¥10.8 million
Employer-side social insurance (~15%) ¥1.62 million
Recruitment cost (annual average) ~¥920,000
Turnover risk cost ~¥1 million
Total reduction ~¥14.34 million+

Additional savings not quantified above: transportation, benefits, management time spent on recruiting, business disruption during vacancies, and repeated turnover risk. The effective total is estimated at approximately ¥16.9 million per year.

Payback period: roughly 1.8 months

The AI accounting system implementation cost, after applying Japan's Digital / AI Adoption Subsidy (50% rate, up to ¥4.5 million), starts at approximately ¥2.5 million.

Against annual savings of approximately ¥16.9 million, the initial investment of ¥2.5 million pays for itself in roughly 1.8 months.

In other words, the investment is recovered in less than two months. From month three onward, the savings flow straight to the bottom line.

The benefit that doesn't show up in the numbers

Cost reduction isn't the only advantage. There's a bigger change that's harder to quantify:

Decision-ready numbers, visible in real time.

Under a traditional accounting setup, accurate numbers don't arrive until the monthly close is done. At many companies, that takes two to three weeks. April's performance isn't visible until mid-May. Every decision runs at least a month behind reality.

With AI accounting, daily transactions are aggregated in real time. Revenue, costs, profit, cash flow — all displayed on a dashboard, accessible anytime.

At OBC, we consolidated the numbers Chairman Kato sees each month into seven groups. "Where the company stands right now" is visible the moment you open the screen. This fundamentally changed the speed and accuracy of management decisions.

What business owners really want isn't "cheap accounting." It's "usable numbers." AI accounting delivers both.

Frequently asked questions

Q.Is ¥16.9 million in annual savings really achievable for most companies?

The figures are based on a model and actual savings depend on company size, transaction volume, and current staffing. The model assumes a mid-market company with a four-person accounting team and 2,500–3,000 monthly journal entries. For a smaller company with a two- or three-person accounting team, savings may be roughly half — though once you factor in tribal knowledge and overtime costs, the effective gain can run higher.

Q.Payback in 1.8 months seems too fast. How does that math work?

The ¥2.5 million initial investment assumes use of Japan's Digital / AI Adoption Subsidy (50% rate, up to ¥4.5 million). Against ¥16.9 million in annual savings, ¥2.5 million pays for itself in roughly 1.8 months. Without the subsidy, the initial investment is approximately ¥5 million and payback extends to about 3.5 months — still within four months.

Q.Is it really feasible for one person to do the work of four?

The one person isn't just "pressing buttons." They verify AI output, handle judgment calls, and process exceptions. At OBC, AI handles 80–90% of journal entries automatically; one person manages audit, confirmation, and exception handling for 2,500–3,000 monthly entries. The labor that disappears is the redundant transcription and clerical work — not the judgment work.

Q.Modern accounting software already has real-time dashboards. What's different here?

Standard accounting software does provide dashboards. The difference is granularity tuned for management decisions. At OBC, we consolidated 162 chart-of-account items into 37 categories, then organized those into seven top-level groups. Redesigning "what numbers the executive actually needs to see" to match each company's decision structure — that's the substance of an AI accounting project.
