Story

Eighty years in Osaka —steel, real estate, and now, something new.

Osaka Hoso's predecessor, Maruichi Kosan Co., Ltd., was founded in 1945 as a steel company in Osaka. From Itachibori to Sakai Sanbo, Edobori, then to Hokkō Tsuneyoshi — moving with the industry, for eighty years the company traded in steel and its alloys, while building a solid foundation in real estate.

Then we met a broadcaster. Radio Osaka (OBC) — on the air since 1958, a public-interest broadcaster that has carried Osaka's airwaves for more than half a century. In a rapidly shifting media landscape, the station needed a new structure for its next era. We didn't just invest and watch from the sidelines. We walked in as members of the team and committed to building that next era together.

In 2026, we changed our company name to Osaka Hoso Co., Ltd. This was not a rebrand. It was a declaration: we staked the name of our 80-year-old enterprise on a commitment to walk forward with Radio Osaka, all in.

Our vision for Radio Osaka is transformation into a content IP studio. Programs that flow through the airwaves and vanish — replaced by content that accumulates as assets the more you create. Starting from radio, building IP that endures and is loved.

We started that transformation at home. We rebuilt Radio Osaka — from operations and accounting to on-air programming — with AI, using our own hands. "The older the organization, the bigger the AI impact." This conviction, forged on the foundation of eighty years of trust, is now our core business: AI/DX transformation for companies.

From steel, to broadcasting, to AI. The shape of the business changes. But we stay rooted in Osaka, and we protect what must be protected, no matter what.

Founder Gentaro Kato — His life, in brief

Born in 1906 in Toyooka, Hyōgo Prefecture. At fifteen, he entered the Osaka steel firm of Yasutaro Kitajima, stepping into the world of iron. Over twenty years under Kitajima, he sharpened his judgment and nerve, and earned a reputation for unwavering loyalty.

In October 1945 — one month after Japan's surrender — he went independent at age thirty-nine. From a single desk, he built Kato Shoten, which he later incorporated as Maruichi Kosan. The company surged through Japan's high-growth era, and Kato established himself as a pillar of the Kansai steel distribution industry. From founding to death, the company never posted a loss.

Stern but paternal with his employees, he cared for his former mentor Yasutaro Kitajima — who had fallen on hard times — for thirty years. Those who knew called it a story of profound gratitude.

He died in 1979, at seventy-three, still serving as president. His legacy passed to his son Katsuhiro, grandson Masataka, and today's leader Yorihiko — and eighty years on, the company now writes its newest chapter as Osaka Hoso.

Records from the founding

Below is a short biography of our founder, Gentaro Kato, who built the steel firm Maruichi Kosan from the ground up after founding it in 1945. It was published in the Spring 1980 issue of the steel-trade journal Tekko Shunju, and is reproduced here, condensed, with the author's permission — preserved as a record of the man who started it all.

A man of rare standing — Gentaro Kato and his singular, charismatic life

Gifted, yet modest by nature

In the high-growth decades after the war, Gentaro Kato (known as Katsumi), founder of Maruichi Kosan Co., Ltd., rose to prominence while remaining a quietly weighty figure in Kansai's steel trade. He died of gallbladder cancer at seventy-three, still the sitting president; many mourned him. He had built an outstanding company out of the postwar chaos, yet never flaunted his sharp mind or iron will. Modest by nature, he won people's trust without seeking it. He was bold and meticulous at once — and from youth carried an almost intuitive, charismatic streak, perceiving and choosing as if by instinct and pressing his will through with intensity.

Into the Kitajima firm after commercial school

Born on New Year's Day, 1906, in Toyooka, Hyogo, Gentaro was raised largely by his grandmother in unsettled family circumstances. In 1920, at just fifteen, he finished commercial school and entered a steel merchant in Osaka, the Kitajima Yasutaro firm — his first step into the world of steel, where he would spend the next two decades.

An insight that stood above the rest

From his years at Kitajima he drew lessons that lasted a lifetime. A colleague, Masajiro Onogi, later remembered him this way:

"Even then, Kato was the third-ranking clerk, in a suit, called 'Genkichi-don.' Sharp, with insight that stood far above the rest — gutsy, fierce, and uncompromising in everything. He said little, sat solid, and fixed you with a swallowing stare; they nicknamed him 'the Toad,' and the younger ones were afraid of him."

Around this time, in his mid-twenties, he took up the Kannon faith — a turn that deepened his already serious, exacting character.

After the war: independent, building the foundation in earnest

Through the war years he was swept through a series of state-controlled steel bodies. In October 1945, in the ruins of Japan's economy, he struck out on his own at thirty-two, opening Kato Shoten — resolved, as he put it, to live by steel and die by steel. For the first years he worked from a single borrowed desk, little more than a middleman. Patience paid: in late 1948 he reorganized the business as Maruichi Kosan Co., Ltd. and became its president. Steadily, over the next decade and through the high-growth years, the firm grew into a leading steel agency in Kansai — and, it is noted, never once closed a year in the red.

A connoisseur in the pursuit of true flavor

Those who knew him — his younger brother Hiroshi, colleagues and friends — describe a man of sincerity, patience and effort: quiet but deeply considered, scrupulously honest, never chasing easy money or empty pleasures. Warm yet careful, he placed the right people in the right roles and raised talent, treating his employees like family while prizing duty, responsibility and the firm's good name. He took no honorary titles and never stood in the spotlight. His one indulgence was fine food — a genuine pursuit of true flavor, especially in fish. After his death, his eldest son Katsuhiro succeeded him as president.

Condensed from Tekko Shunju, Spring issue (April 15, 1980).
